# Myanmar: International Briefing

*Report date: December 19, 2024*

Myanmar's military leadership has undergone significant restructuring as junta chief Min Aung Hlaing was nominated for the presidency by the military-controlled parliament [3]. The move appears designed to consolidate Min Aung Hlaing's authority and potentially address the regime's diplomatic isolation [3]. As part of this transition, Min Aung Hlaing transferred military command to a close ally while moving toward the presidential role [3]. The political maneuvering occurs amid sustained armed resistance, with anti-junta forces engaged in continued fighting in northern regions, particularly around Mogok in Shan State, where villages have been evacuated in anticipation of military assaults [3].

The Myanmar National Democratic Alliance Army has expanded its control over northern Shan State trade corridors, representing a significant shift in commercial route management with implications for cross-border trade flows [3]. Despite fuel shortages affecting other sectors, the Myanmar military maintains daily aerial bombing campaigns across multiple regions [3]. These developments indicate ongoing challenges to the military government's territorial control and economic leverage.

Myanmar's economy faces severe disruption from multiple concurrent crises. The Middle East conflict has disrupted transportation networks and created fuel shortages that are driving up costs for food distribution and agricultural production [1]. Fertilizer prices are expected to double compared to the previous year, threatening farmers preparing for monsoon crops [1]. These external shocks compound existing economic pressures in a country where basic infrastructure and supply chains remain compromised by ongoing conflict.

The food security crisis has reached alarming proportions, with 12.4 million people facing acute hunger [1]. Agricultural sectors face particular strain as global instability affects commodity prices and supply chains, placing additional pressure on households already struggling with basic necessities [1]. The combination of fuel price increases and ongoing conflict creates compounding effects on vulnerable populations across multiple states.

One year following the devastating earthquake that struck Sagaing and Mandalay regions, recovery remains fragile. One in six households in earthquake-affected areas continue experiencing moderate to severe food insecurity, whilst half of all families remain marginally food secure [1]. The World Food Programme has transitioned from emergency relief operations to infrastructure restoration programs aimed at long-term community stability [1]. The organisation requires $150 million to assist 1.5 million people with humanitarian support [1].

Myanmar's position as a crossroads between South Asia and mainland Southeast Asia continues to shape its cultural identity [4]. The country shares borders with Thailand, China, India, Bangladesh, and Laos, whilst maintaining a long coastline along the Bay of Bengal and the Andaman Sea [4]. This geographic positioning historically influences trade patterns, cultural exchange, and settlement development across the region.
