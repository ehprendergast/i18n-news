Russia's economic indicators present a concerning picture of financial strain across multiple sectors. Overdue wage debts reached 2.01 billion rubles by the end of February 2026, marking an 8.3% monthly increase of 153.9 million rubles [11]. The year-on-year comparison reveals a stark deterioration, with wage debts growing by 70% compared to February 2025 [11]. The temporal distribution of these arrears shows that 65.6% originated from 2025, while 16.6% stemmed from earlier periods, and current-year debt accounts for 17.8% of the total outstanding amount [11].

The sectoral breakdown of wage arrears reveals significant concentration in key industries. The construction sector bears the heaviest burden, accounting for 42.7% of total wage arrears [11]. Manufacturing follows as the second-most affected sector at 25.2% of total arrears [11], while the mining sector represents 14.2% of outstanding wage debts [11]. Nearly all wage debts stem from enterprises lacking sufficient funds rather than budget shortfalls, with enterprise funding shortfall debts totalling 2.005 billion rubles, representing an 8.1% increase from January [11].

Government payment systems show varying performance across administrative levels. Federal and regional budget wage payments showed no arrears by February's end [11], while local budget wage debts stood at 4.5 million rubles [11]. This distinction highlights the concentrated nature of payment difficulties at the enterprise level rather than systematic government budget failures.

Russia's monetary reserves experienced significant changes during early 2026. The Central Bank reduced gold reserves by 500,000 troy ounces over January-February 2026, representing the fastest pace of decline since 2015 [12]. The reduction occurred in two phases: 300,000 troy ounces in January and 200,000 troy ounces in February [12]. These sales totalled approximately 15.55 tonnes of gold [12], though this represented less than 1% of total monetary gold reserves [12].

The gold reserve drawdown brought Russia's holdings to 74.3 million troy ounces, marking the lowest level since March 2022 [12]. Despite these reductions, Russia maintains its fifth-place position globally in gold reserves, ranking behind the United States, Germany, Italy, and France [12]. Gold reserve valuations peaked at $402.7 billion in January but declined by $18.681 billion (4.6%) to $384 billion in February [12].

The broader impact on Russia's international reserves was substantial, with total reserves contracting by $24.264 billion (2.9%) to $809.308 billion during February [12]. The Central Bank maintains operational secrecy regarding its gold transactions, declining to disclose details about purchases, sales, volumes, pricing, market segments, or transaction timing [12].
