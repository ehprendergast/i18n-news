The Indian government has launched the Bal Vivah Mukt Bharat initiative, aimed at eliminating child marriages across the country by 2030 [2]. This mission, introduced by the Ministry of Women and Child Development in November 2024, aligns with constitutional Article 21 provisions and the Prohibition of Child Marriage Act of 2006, targeting practices that disproportionately affect girls from marginalised communities [2]. The initiative forms part of the broader vision for a developed India by 2047, emphasising equal participation of women and girls across all spheres of life [2].

India's constitutional framework, established within four years of independence in 1947, sought to integrate diverse linguistic regions and religious communities into a unified nation-state while maintaining substantial autonomy for individual states [3]. The governance structure, built on citizen choice and featuring legislative, executive, and judicial branches, was implemented before similar systems gained international recognition [3]. Jawaharlal Nehru served as the country's first Prime Minister, with Rajendra Prasad as the inaugural President [3].

Agricultural development remains central to India's economic planning, with the country achieving consistent annual growth rates of three percent in agricultural production through scientific research and development [3]. Land reforms, improved marketing techniques, and enhanced irrigation facilities have been prioritised to reduce dependency on monsoon rainfall, given that most Indian agriculture remains rain-fed [3]. The Northern Plains, formed by the Indus, Ganges, and Brahmaputra river systems, constitute the country's primary agricultural hub as a fertile alluvial plain [4].

India occupies 3,287,263 square kilometres, making it the seventh-largest country by area and second-most populous globally [4]. The nation borders Afghanistan and Pakistan to the north, China, Nepal, and Bhutan to the north and northeast, and Bangladesh and Myanmar to the east, with the Indian Ocean surrounding its southwestern and southern coastlines [4]. The country divides into 28 states and 8 Union Territories, encompassing four distinct geographical regions: the Himalayan Region, Northern Plains, Peninsular Plateau, and Coastal Plains [4].

Cultural practices emphasise physical purity and spiritual refinement, with fatalism widespread as a component of traditional belief systems [1]. Family structures prioritise collective needs over individual preferences, with families generally large despite government encouragement of family planning [1]. Traditional attire includes the saree for women, a long fabric draped in various ways, though urban areas increasingly reflect Western influences [1].

Cricket and films constitute India's primary recreational passions, with cricket introduced during British colonial rule as a slow-paced game where matches can extend over multiple days [1]. Regional dietary variations are significant, with rice serving as a staple in southern regions while other grains dominate elsewhere [1]. Modern eating habits often involve families dining together, with many using the right hand rather than utensils for consumption [1].

Dating practices from Western countries remain uncommon, though urban residents increasingly adopt Western standards [1]. Traditional marriages continue to predominate over contemporary courtship methods [1]. Social visits typically occur in homes, with unannounced visits between friends and family members being common practice [1].
