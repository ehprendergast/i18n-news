Cricket remains India's dominant sporting passion, with matches capable of lasting several days according to traditional formats introduced during British colonial rule [1]. The sport continues to attract widespread national attention alongside India's substantial film industry, which constitutes one of the country's twin recreational obsessions [1].

Regional dietary practices reflect India's cultural diversity, with rice serving as the primary staple in southern states while northern regions favour different grain-based foods [1]. Traditional eating customs persist in many households, where families consume meals together using their right hand rather than utensils [1]. Women's traditional dress includes the saree, a long fabric draped in various regional styles [1].

India occupies 3,287,263 square kilometres, making it the seventh largest country by area globally [4]. The nation's geography divides into four distinct regions: the Himalayan mountains in the north, the fertile Northern Plains formed by major river systems, the mineral-rich Peninsular Plateau, and the Coastal Plains stretching along both eastern and western seaboards [4]. Mount Kanchenjunga represents India's highest peak at 8,598 metres [4].

The country achieved independence at midnight on 15 August 1947, with Jawaharlal Nehru becoming the first Prime Minister and Rajendra Prasad the first President [3]. India's Constitution, completed within four years of independence, established governance structures incorporating the Legislature, Executive, and Judiciary while granting substantial autonomy to diverse states within the Indian Union [3]. The administrative structure comprises 28 states and 8 Union Territories [4]. The government launched the Bal Vivah Mukt Bharat initiative in November 2024, targeting the elimination of child marriages by 2030 in alignment with Sustainable Development Goal 5.3 [2]. This programme operates under constitutional provisions including Article 21 and the Prohibition of Child Marriage Act of 2006 [2].
