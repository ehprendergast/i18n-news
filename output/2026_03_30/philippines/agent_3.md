The Philippines faces significant energy security challenges as President Ferdinand Marcos Jr. declared a national energy emergency in response to disruptions from Middle East conflicts [9]. The emergency declaration grants the government authority to control fuel prices and expedite imports from alternative suppliers, including Russia [9]. Philippine authorities report maintaining fuel reserves sufficient for approximately 45 days at standard consumption levels [9].

The country's energy vulnerability stems from its dependence on oil imports through the Strait of Hormuz, which has experienced closures due to regional warfare [9]. The strait facilitates transport of roughly one-fifth of global crude oil supply, with approximately 80% destined for Asian markets [9]. Oil prices have risen sharply following the waterway's disruption, prompting the International Energy Agency to characterize the current crisis as exceeding the combined impact of 1970s energy shocks [9].

The Philippine archipelago consists of over 7,600 islands, with the majority of its fast-growing population concentrated on eleven main islands [8]. The country experiences frequent natural disasters, including earthquakes and volcanic eruptions from approximately twenty active volcanoes, alongside regular typhoons and storms [8]. Manila serves as the capital, with the national population reaching 109.035 million [8].

The archipelago's strategic location places it in Southeast Asia, bordered by the Pacific Ocean and South China Sea, positioned below Taiwan, east of Vietnam, and north of Indonesia [10]. Historical influences include three centuries of Spanish colonial rule followed by control in the early 20th century, leading to independence in 1946 under a constitution modeled after governance structures [8]. The country operates as the Republic of the Philippines, with Filipino and English as official languages alongside regional languages [8].
