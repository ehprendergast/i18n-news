Finland's governing right-wing coalition moved to dismantle a Cold War-era legal prohibition on nuclear weapons, proposing amendments to both the Nuclear Energy Act and the Criminal Code [3]. Defence Minister Antti Häkkänen announced that the 1980s-era blanket ban—which criminalised the import, transport, possession, manufacture, and detonation of nuclear devices—was no longer compatible with Finland's obligations as a NATO member, which it joined following Russia's full-scale invasion of Ukraine in 2022 [1]. The proposed amendments would permit nuclear weapons in the context of Finland's homeland defence, NATO collective defence, or defence cooperation, while retaining criminal prohibitions on manufacture and detonation [3]. President Alexander Stubb defended the move as necessary to remove "law-based obstacles hampering the defence and deterrence by NATO" [2]. The government does not seek to host nuclear weapons, and NATO has indicated no such deployment is planned [2].

The proposal has generated considerable parliamentary and civil-society opposition. The main opposition Social Democratic Party (SDP), the Green League, and the Left Alliance all registered objections [2]. SDP chair Antti Lindtman argued the change would distance Finland from the nuclear policies of its Nordic neighbours, while SDP lawmaker Tytti Tuppurainen contended the amendment goes further than Sweden, Denmark, or Norway, which maintain political positions—rather than legal bans—against nuclear weapons in peacetime [3]. Tytti Erästö of SIPRI noted that Finland had been the only Nordic country with a full statutory prohibition [2]. Laura Lodenius of the Peace Union disputed the security rationale [2]. Opposition leaders were informed of the decision only one day before the public announcement, a timeline critics described as insufficient for deliberation [2]. Draft amendments entered public consultation with comments due by 2 April [2].

On the diplomatic front, Prime Minister Orpo was scheduled to meet with Canadian and Nordic counterparts in Norway [4], while President Stubb concluded a state visit to India during which Finland-India relations were elevated to a "Strategic Partnership" and Stubb co-inaugurated the 11th Raisina Dialogue alongside Prime Minister Modi [5]. NATO AWACS aircraft conducted their first airborne surveillance mission in Finnish airspace, marking a further integration of Finnish territory into alliance operational frameworks [5]. A first repatriation flight from the Middle East arrived at Helsinki Airport during the period [5].

On economic matters, the government submitted its budget proposal for 2026 to parliament [4]. The Helsinki Energy Group announced plans for substantial investment in small modular nuclear reactors [5]. Finnish biopharmaceutical company Faron confirmed a €40 million investment round to fund a blood cancer trial [5]. The Helsinki Regional Transport Authority (HSL) disclosed potential layoffs, reflecting continued fiscal pressure on public transport operations [5]. Finnish aviation recorded gradual recovery in passenger volumes [5].

Statistics Finland data covering 2024 indicated that more than one-third of adults up to age 29 were at risk of poverty or social marginalisation, representing an increase of approximately 56,000 individuals compared with 2023 and a rise of nearly ten percentage points since 2018 [7]. Across all ages, approximately 958,000 people—roughly one in six of Finland's 5.66 million residents—were classified as at risk [7]. Risk levels increased among those aged 30 to 49 and were particularly acute among adults 35 and under living alone, 54 percent of whom fell into the at-risk category [7]. By contrast, risk levels among seniors declined over the same period [7].

Finland retained its designation as the world's happiest country, a status Aalto University's Professor Frank Martela attributed to the Nordic welfare model [6]. A separate Aalto study of nearly 40,000 children found that extending pre-school from one to two years produced no measurable improvement in school-age skills [6]. Finnish cities were identified as European leaders in urban tree canopy cover, with research linking the phenomenon to reduced heatwave effects and improved wellbeing [6]. Over 720,000 Finns—approximately one in eight—are regular cold-water swimmers [6].

In the arts, Marjo Smolander's exhibition "Cosmologies," a collaborative project between Finnish and African cultural practitioners, attracted attention during the period [5]. Architect Jaakko Torvinen's Puusauna, a wood-fired sauna structure, received Wallpaper Magazine's Life Enhancer of the Year Award [6]. Aalto University's Laboratory of Hope exhibition continued on campus through 27 March [6].

Approximately 20,000 people marched in Helsinki to mark International Women's Day [5]. In sport, a Finnish pair won the UK Wife Carrying Race, an annual competition with roots in Finnish tradition [5].
