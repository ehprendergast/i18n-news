Finland's government moved in March 2026 to dismantle a legislative prohibition on nuclear weapons that has stood since the 1980s. Defence Minister Antti Häkkänen announced that the Nuclear Energy Act and Criminal Code would be amended to permit the import, transport, and storage of nuclear weapons on Finnish territory [1]. The current law, passed in 1987, categorically bans the import, transport, possession, manufacture, and detonation of nuclear devices [3]. The proposed amendments would allow nuclear weapons in the context of homeland defence, NATO collective defence, or defence cooperation agreements, while manufacturing and detonation would remain criminal offences [3]. Häkkänen argued that the existing legislation "does not meet the needs that Finland has as a NATO member" [1], a status Finland acquired following Russia's full-scale invasion of Ukraine in 2022. Finland shares more than 1,000 kilometres of border with Russia [1]. President Alexander Stubb defended the proposal as necessary to ensure "no law-based obstacles hampering the defense and deterrence by NATO" [2].

The proposal has triggered sustained parliamentary opposition. The Social Democratic Party, the Green League, and the Left Alliance have all voiced objections [2]. SDP chair Antti Lindtman argued the move would distance Finland from the nuclear policies of its Nordic neighbours, while SDP lawmaker Tytti Tuppurainen contended the amendment goes further than stated, effectively removing all restrictions on nuclear weapons [3]. Critics also challenged the process: opposition leaders were reportedly informed only one day before the announcement [2]. Häkkänen acknowledged the limited prior communication, citing sensitivity [2]. Tytti Erästö of the Stockholm International Peace Research Institute observed that while Finland is the only Nordic country with a full legal ban, Sweden, Denmark, and Norway maintain strong political positions against nuclear weapons in peacetime — though these are not encoded in law [2][3]. Laura Lodenius of the Finnish Peace Union said the change would not make Finland safer [2]. Draft amendments entered public consultation, with a comment deadline of 2 April [2]. The Finnish government has stated it does not seek to host nuclear weapons and that NATO has no plans for such a deployment [2].

On defence and diplomacy, NATO AWACS aircraft conducted their first airborne surveillance mission in Finnish airspace during the period [5]. Prime Minister Orpo was scheduled to meet Canadian and Nordic counterparts in Norway [4]. President Stubb concluded a state visit to India, with the two countries elevating bilateral ties to a "Strategic Partnership"; Stubb and Prime Minister Modi co-inaugurated the 11th Raisina Dialogue [5].

Finland's poverty statistics present a less settled picture than its reputation for social cohesion might suggest. According to Statistics Finland, the number of young adults at risk of poverty or social marginalisation rose sharply in 2024, with more than a third of adults up to age 29 now classified as at risk — an increase of 56,000 compared with 2023 [7]. The share of this cohort at risk has grown by nearly ten percentage points since 2018 [7]. Overall, 958,000 people out of a population of roughly 5.66 million are at risk of poverty or exclusion [7]. Among people aged 35 and under living in single-person households, 54 per cent face such risks [7]. Risks also rose among the 30-to-49 age group, while declining among seniors [7]. Separately, Helsinki Regional Transport Authority HSL announced potential staff layoffs [5].

In the health and investment sector, the Finnish biotech firm Faron confirmed a €40 million investment round connected to a blood cancer trial [5]. Helsinki Energy Group announced plans to invest in small modular nuclear reactors [5]. The Finnish aviation sector recorded gradual recovery [5].

Finland retained its position atop the World Happiness Report rankings [6]. Professor Frank Martela of Aalto University attributed the outcome to Finland's Nordic welfare model [6]. Research published by Professor Matti Sarvimäki, drawing on a study of 40,000 children, found that extending pre-school from one to two years did not improve school-age academic skills [6]. Over 720,000 Finns — approximately one in eight — are regular cold-water swimmers [6]. Finnish cities were identified as European leaders in tree canopy cover [6].

In culture, Marjo Smolander's exhibition "Cosmologies," a collaboration between Finnish and African artists, drew attention in Helsinki [5]. Architect Jaakko Torvinen's Puusauna installation received Wallpaper Magazine's Life Enhancer of the Year Award [6]. Aalto University's Laboratory of Hope exhibition ran through 27 March 2026 [6]. Some 15,000 to 20,000 people marched in Helsinki for International Women's Day [5]. A Finnish pair won the UK Wife Carrying Race, an event with origins in Finnish folk tradition [5].

- The proposal faces opposition from SDP, the Greens, and the Left Alliance, with critics citing both the policy substance and the limited advance consultation with opposition leaders.
