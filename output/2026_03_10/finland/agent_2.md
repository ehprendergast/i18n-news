Finland's governing right-wing coalition moved this week to dismantle a Cold War-era legislative prohibition on nuclear weapons, proposing amendments to both the Nuclear Energy Act and the Criminal Code [3]. Under legislation passed in 1987, the import, transport, possession, manufacture, and detonation of nuclear devices was categorically prohibited [3]. The proposed draft would permit nuclear weapons in the context of Finland's homeland defense, NATO's collective defense, and defense cooperation, while retaining criminal penalties for manufacturing or detonating such weapons [3]. Defense Minister Antti Häkkänen told a press conference that the existing ban "does not meet the needs that Finland has as a NATO member," citing the country's more than 1,000-kilometer border with Russia and the altered security environment following Moscow's full-scale invasion of Ukraine [1]. The ruling coalition commands a parliamentary majority, and draft amendments entered public consultation with comments due by April 2 [2][3].

The proposal drew immediate domestic opposition. The main opposition Social Democratic Party (SDP), the Green League, and the Left Alliance all voiced objections [2]. SDP chair Antti Lindtman said the change would distance Finland from the nuclear policies of its Nordic neighbors, while SDP lawmaker Tytti Tuppurainen argued the amendment goes further than those neighbors by removing all restrictions rather than maintaining political prohibitions [2][3]. Tytti Erästö of the Stockholm International Peace Research Institute noted that while Finland is the only Nordic country with a full legal ban, Sweden, Denmark, and Norway maintain strong political positions — not laws — against nuclear weapons [2][3]. Laura Lodenius of the Peace Union warned the change would not make Finland safer [2]. Critics also questioned the process, noting that opposition leaders were informed only one day in advance [2]. President Alexander Stubb defended the move as necessary to ensure "no law-based obstacles hampering the defense and deterrence by NATO," and the government stated that Finland does not seek to host nuclear weapons and that NATO has no such plans [2]. Minister Häkkänen cited the sensitivity of the matter to explain limited prior public communication [2].

On the international diplomatic front, President Stubb concluded a visit to India, during which Finland-India ties were elevated to a "Strategic Partnership" [5]. Stubb and Prime Minister Narendra Modi jointly inaugurated the 11th Raisina Dialogue, an annual geopolitical conference hosted in New Delhi [5]. Prime Minister Petteri Orpo was separately scheduled to meet with Canadian and Nordic prime ministers in Norway [4]. NATO AWACS aircraft conducted their first airborne surveillance mission in Finnish airspace, marking a milestone in Finland's integration into the alliance's collective defense architecture [5].

On fiscal matters, the government submitted its budget proposal for 2026 to Parliament [4]. Helsinki Energy Group announced plans to invest substantially in small modular nuclear reactors [5]. Helsinki Regional Transport Authority HSL signaled potential workforce reductions [5]. The Finnish aviation sector recorded gradual recovery in passenger volumes [5]. In the healthcare investment space, Faron Pharmaceuticals confirmed a €40 million investment round to fund a blood cancer trial [5].

Statistics Finland data for 2024 showed an increasing share of young adults at risk of poverty or social marginalisation [7]. More than a third of adults up to age 29 were in this category, representing an increase of approximately 56,000 individuals compared to 2023 and a rise of nearly 10 percentage points since 2018 [7]. Among people aged 35 and under living in single-person households, 54 percent were at risk [7]. Overall, approximately 958,000 people — roughly one in six of Finland's population of 5.66 million — were at risk of poverty or social marginalisation in 2024 [7]. Risk levels rose among the 30-49 age cohort while declining among seniors [7].

Finland retained its standing as the world's happiest country, a status attributed by Aalto University's Professor Frank Martela to the Nordic welfare model [6]. Research highlighted Finnish cities as European leaders in tree canopy cover, with studies indicating urban trees mitigate heatwaves and support physical and mental wellbeing [6]. A separate Aalto University study of nearly 40,000 children found that extending pre-school education from one to two years did not improve school-age skills, a finding likely to inform ongoing policy debate [6]. Over 720,000 Finns — approximately one in eight — are regular cold-water swimmers [6]. Architect Jaakko Torvinen's Puusauna received Wallpaper Magazine's Life Enhancer of the Year Award [6].

In the arts, an exhibition titled "Marjo Smolander's Cosmologies," described as a collaboration between Finnish and African cultures, was among cultural events noted during the period [5]. The Laboratory of Hope exhibition continued on the Aalto University campus through March 27, 2026 [6]. A first repatriation flight from the Middle East arrived at Helsinki Airport [5].

An estimated 15,000 to 20,000 people marched in Helsinki to mark International Women's Day [5]. In sport, a Finnish pair won the UK Wife Carrying Race, an annual competition held in Dorking, Surrey, which derives from a Finnish tradition originating in Sonkajärvi [5].
