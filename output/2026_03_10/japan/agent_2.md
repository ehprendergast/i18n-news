Japan's parliament, the Diet, convened in February 2026 under new political conditions following a snap general election on February 8 [1]. Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi's Liberal Democratic Party coalition secured 352 of 465 lower-house seats, the party's strongest electoral result since its founding in 1955 [1]. Takaichi, Japan's first female prime minister, campaigned on a platform of tax reduction and economic stimulus, citing household financial pressure as the central domestic concern [1]. The scale of the victory grants her coalition legislative room to advance defence-spending expansion and structural economic reform without reliance on coalition bargaining [1].

Takaichi's defence agenda is taking concrete form amid sharpened tensions with China [2]. Tokyo announced plans to deploy advanced missile systems in prefectures proximate to Taiwan, a move framed by officials as a necessary deterrent adjustment [2]. In the East China Sea, Japanese coast guard authorities boarded and seized a Chinese fishing vessel, arresting its captain on charges of defying inspection orders [2]. Beijing responded through its Commerce Ministry, restricting exports of "dual-use" goods to twenty Japanese firms on national-security grounds [2]. The dispute has extended to the symbolic: China declined to renew the loan of its remaining two giant pandas housed in Japanese zoos, and the animals were returned, closing a chapter of panda diplomacy that had spanned decades [2].

Japan's fourth-quarter 2025 GDP growth was revised sharply upward to an annualised 1.3%, well above the preliminary estimate of 0.2% [3]. Business investment rose 1.3%, the strongest quarterly reading since the final quarter of 2023, while private consumption increased 0.3% [3]. Domestic demand contributed 0.3 percentage points to headline growth; external demand contributed nothing [3]. Nominal GDP reached 663.8 trillion yen, or approximately $4.2 trillion [3]. The result follows a 2.6% contraction in the third quarter, and economists caution that January household spending subsequently fell 1.0% year-on-year, complicating any narrative of durable recovery [3].

The Bank of Japan held its policy rate at 0.75% at its January meeting [4]. Governor Kazuo Ueda revised inflation forecasts upward, while Deputy Governor Himino indicated that ongoing conflict involving Iran had not altered the BOJ's monetary stance [4]. Energy price volatility, stemming partly from Middle East instability, continues to complicate the BOJ's tightening trajectory [4][5]. The IMF urged Japan to maintain a path of sustained rate increases; small businesses, however, are facing insolvency pressure from inflation that wages have not matched [4][7]. A noted tension persists between the BOJ's inclination toward gradual tightening and Prime Minister Takaichi's publicly stated preference for lower borrowing costs [4].

Energy supply security has risen to ministerial priority following crude oil price increases, with West Texas Intermediate exceeding $110 per barrel [5]. Japan imports more than 90% of its crude oil from the Middle East, the bulk of which transits the Strait of Hormuz [5]. The Ministry of Economy, Trade and Industry instructed operators to prepare emergency reserves; Japan currently holds approximately 254 days of supply [5]. Mitsubishi Chemical announced cuts to ethylene production after naphtha supplies — roughly 40% of which originate from the Middle East — were disrupted [5]. Prime Minister Takaichi is considering implementing retail gasoline price controls, and Japan has joined G7 coordination efforts regarding strategic stockpile releases [5].

Japanese industry is simultaneously navigating labour scarcity and structural sectoral investment [6]. Major commercial banks, including Mizuho and Sumitomo Mitsui Banking Corporation, are establishing dedicated semiconductor financing divisions as part of a broader $50 billion domestic chip industry push [6]. Heavy manufacturers IHI and Mitsubishi Heavy Industries are scaling aircraft engine maintenance capacity, while Komatsu and Hitachi Construction are sustaining price increases on equipment [6]. JR East has begun training foreign nationals for rail operations roles, a direct response to chronic skilled-worker shortages across transport infrastructure [6]. Vestas has commenced wind turbine manufacturing in Japan, and Nissan has announced a self-driving vehicle partnership with Uber [6]. Ajinomoto is introducing Thai and Brazilian product lines to the Japanese domestic market, and Proterial has developed magnets that eliminate dependence on heavy rare-earth inputs [6].

A loneliness epidemic, documented by government surveys and civil society researchers, is drawing sustained policy attention [7]. Wages across most sectors have failed to keep pace with consumer price increases, eroding household purchasing power even as nominal economic indicators have improved [7][3]. The return of the giant pandas from Ueno and Kobe zoos to China, while formally a matter of loan-term expiry, was widely read in domestic media as a marker of deteriorating bilateral sentiment with Beijing [2][7].
