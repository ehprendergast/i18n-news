Japan's political landscape shifted decisively on 8 February 2026, when Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi led the Liberal Democratic Party coalition to 352 of 465 seats in a snap election — the party's strongest result since its founding in 1955 [1]. Takaichi, Japan's first female prime minister, campaigned on a platform of economic reform and defense expansion, securing what she described as a strong mandate for a conservative agenda including tax reductions and fiscal stimulus [1]. The scale of the victory has consolidated her authority within the LDP and positioned her to advance policies that had previously faced internal resistance, including her stated preference for accommodative monetary conditions [4].

The Bank of Japan held its benchmark rate at 0.75% at its most recent meeting, with Governor Kazuo Ueda revising inflation forecasts upward while Deputy Governor Himino indicated that escalating tensions in the Persian Gulf would not alter the rate trajectory [4]. The IMF has called on Tokyo to sustain rate increases, placing it at odds with the Takaichi government's preference for looser policy [4]. Small businesses continue to bear disproportionate pressure from inflation-driven cost increases, with a rise in insolvencies reported among firms unable to pass on higher input costs [4]. Japan's Q4 2025 GDP grew at an annualized rate of 1.3%, a marked upward revision from the preliminary estimate of 0.2%, with business investment rising 1.3% — its strongest quarterly gain since Q4 2023 [3]. Private consumption added 0.3%, though external demand contributed nothing to the headline figure, and nominal GDP reached 663.8 trillion yen ($4.2 trillion) [3]. January household spending fell 1.0% year-on-year, a cautionary reading following Q3's contraction of 2.6% [3].

The energy situation presents an acute policy challenge. Iran's declaration of closure in the Strait of Hormuz has focused attention on Japan's exposure: over 90% of its crude oil imports transit the waterway [5]. The Ministry of Economy, Trade and Industry is preparing to release strategic petroleum reserves, with Japan holding sufficient supply for roughly 254 days [5]. WTI crude has exceeded $110 per barrel, prompting Mitsubishi Chemical to reduce ethylene output — roughly 40% of the feedstock for which derives from Middle Eastern naphtha [5]. Prime Minister Takaichi is considering a cap on gasoline retail prices, and coordination among G7 partners is underway [5]. Against this backdrop, corporate Japan has moved to diversify critical supply chains: Proterial has developed heavy-rare-earth-free magnets, Japan has concluded a deal with Lynas for rare earth supply and is facilitating technology transfer to Malaysia, while Mizuho and SMBC have announced entry into the domestic semiconductor sector, a market valued at some $50 billion [6]. Komatsu and Hitachi have maintained elevated pricing on industrial equipment despite demand-side resistance, and IHI and Mitsubishi Heavy Industries are deepening collaboration on aircraft engine development [6].

Japan's bilateral relationship with China has deteriorated further. Beijing restricted dual-use exports to twenty Japanese firms and, in a gesture freighted with symbolism, the last two giant pandas held in Japanese zoos were returned to China amid the diplomatic chill [2] [7]. Japanese authorities meanwhile seized a Chinese fishing vessel, and the Takaichi government has proceeded with plans to install missile systems in the southwestern islands proximate to Taiwan [2]. Ajinomoto's move to import food brands from Thailand and Brazil reflects a broader commercial reorientation away from supply-chain dependence on China, extending into consumer goods [6]. JR East has launched a foreign worker training programme, signaling continued reliance on immigration as a partial remedy for acute labour shortfalls [6]. Nissan and Uber have announced a collaboration on autonomous vehicle deployment, a sector in which domestic manufacturers have sought to reclaim competitive ground [6].

Japanese society continues to grapple with a deepening loneliness epidemic, with a growing share of the population turning to online platforms for mental health support as institutional and community resources remain insufficient [7]. Wage growth has failed to keep pace with inflation in real terms, sustaining a prolonged squeeze on household purchasing power that sits uneasily alongside the macroeconomic recovery narrative [7]. The return of the giant pandas — once a feature of public affection and soft diplomatic signalling — was observed domestically as an emblem of the broader deterioration in Sino-Japanese relations [2] [7]. Vestas wind turbines are now operational at sites in Japan, marking a notable instance of European clean-energy infrastructure entering a market long dominated by domestic suppliers [6].
