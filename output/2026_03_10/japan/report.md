# Japan: International Briefing

*Report date: 10 March 2026*

Japan enters a period of unusual political coherence after Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi's Liberal Democratic Party coalition secured 352 of 465 seats in the February 8 snap election — its strongest parliamentary majority since 1955 [1]. Takaichi, Japan's first female prime minister, has moved quickly to define an agenda centered on economic reform, tax reduction, and a substantial expansion of defense capacity [1]. The mandate is historically large; the question is whether the structural constraints Japan faces — demographic, fiscal, and geopolitical — will prove amenable to legislative force.

The defense dimension of Takaichi's agenda is already materializing in operational terms. Plans are advancing to deploy missile systems near Taiwan, a move that formalizes Japan's posture in the western Pacific as a frontline concern rather than a secondary one [2]. On the maritime frontier, Japan seized a Chinese fishing vessel and arrested its captain, a confrontation that sits within a pattern of incremental assertion rather than crisis [2]. China's response has included restricting dual-use exports to twenty Japanese firms, a targeted economic measure that suggests Beijing is calibrating pressure rather than escalating broadly [2]. The symbolic dimension has not been absent: the last two giant pandas in Japan have been returned to China, a gesture that both governments understand to carry diplomatic weight [2,7].

GDP data for the fourth quarter of 2025 offers a more encouraging picture than initially reported. The annualized growth rate was revised sharply upward to 1.3% from a preliminary estimate of 0.2%, a revision driven primarily by business investment, which rose 1.3% in its strongest quarter since late 2023 [3]. Private consumption contributed modestly at 0.3%, while external demand made no net contribution [3]. Nominal GDP stood at 663.8 trillion yen, equivalent to approximately $4.2 trillion [3]. The revision is welcome, but context is necessary: the fourth quarter followed a third-quarter contraction of 2.6%, and January household spending fell 1.0% year-on-year, suggesting consumer caution has not dissipated [3].

The Bank of Japan held its policy rate at 0.75% at its most recent meeting, with Governor Kazuo Ueda revising inflation forecasts upward [4]. Deputy Governor Himino indicated that the central bank's stance would remain unchanged in response to the Iran conflict and its energy market consequences [4]. The IMF has urged the BOJ to pursue sustained rate increases, a position broadly consistent with orthodox inflation management [4]. Takaichi's publicly stated preference for lower rates places her in direct tension with the BOJ's current trajectory, a friction that markets and analysts are monitoring closely [4]. Small businesses are already confronting the convergence of elevated inflation and tighter credit conditions, with bankruptcy rates rising in that segment [4].

The energy picture represents perhaps the most acute short-term economic vulnerability. West Texas Intermediate crude has exceeded $110 per barrel, and Japan sources more than 90% of its crude oil via the Strait of Hormuz [5]. The Ministry of Economy, Trade and Industry is preparing for a release of oil reserves, with 254 days of petroleum stockpile available — a substantial buffer by international standards [5]. Japan is coordinating with G7 partners on strategic reserve deployment [5]. The industrial exposure is direct: Mitsubishi Chemical is cutting ethylene production, given that roughly 40% of its naphtha supply originates in the Middle East [5]. Takaichi is considering retail gasoline price controls, an interventionist option that reflects the political sensitivity of energy costs for households [5].

Corporate Japan is navigating the energy and geopolitical environment while pursuing longer-term structural repositioning. Mizuho Financial Group and Sumitomo Mitsui Banking Corporation are establishing dedicated semiconductor divisions to support what has become a $50 billion domestic industry initiative [6]. In materials and manufacturing, Proterial has developed magnets free of heavy rare earth elements, reducing exposure to Chinese supply chains [6]. Japan has concluded a rare earth supply agreement with Lynas Rare Earths, including technology transfer arrangements with Malaysia, further diversifying away from single-source dependency [6]. IHI and Mitsubishi Heavy Industries are expanding aircraft engine maintenance capacity, while Komatsu and Hitachi are sustaining price increases across their equipment lines [6].

Adaptations to structural labor shortages are visible across sectors. JR East has begun training foreign rail workers, a practical acknowledgment that domestic labor supply cannot meet operational requirements [6]. Nissan has entered a self-driving vehicle partnership with Uber, positioning for autonomous mobility as a medium-term hedge against workforce constraints [6]. Ajinomoto is introducing Thai and Brazilian product lines to the Japanese market, a sign that the food industry is recalibrating for both demographic change and shifting consumer tastes [6]. Vestas wind turbines have entered the Japanese market as the energy transition creates new industrial entrants [6].

The social substrate of this economic transformation carries signs of strain. Real wages have failed to keep pace with inflation, eroding the living standards of ordinary households even as corporate earnings remain elevated in export-oriented sectors [7]. The loneliness epidemic, a phenomenon Japanese policymakers have formally acknowledged in recent years, is intensifying, with growing reliance on online mental health platforms as a substitute for institutional and community support structures [7]. The return of the giant pandas — a minor diplomatic episode in strictly material terms — carried outsized public resonance, reflecting an ambient anxiety about Japan's regional relationships that policy documents do not fully capture [2,7].

## Key Developments

- PM Takaichi's LDP coalition holds 352/465 seats following the February 8 snap election, the largest majority since 1955; Takaichi is Japan's first female prime minister [1]
- Japan is advancing missile deployments near Taiwan amid escalating maritime and diplomatic friction with China, including a vessel seizure and targeted Chinese export restrictions on Japanese firms [2]
- Q4 2025 GDP revised to 1.3% annualized growth on strong business investment, reversing a Q3 contraction of 2.6%, though January household spending remained negative [3]
- The Bank of Japan held rates at 0.75% with upward inflation revisions; tension persists between BOJ tightening direction and Takaichi's stated rate preferences [4]
- WTI crude above $110/barrel has triggered contingency planning for strategic reserve release; Japan's near-total Hormuz dependency for crude imports presents a structural vulnerability [5]
- Mizuho and SMBC are creating semiconductor divisions; Proterial and Lynas deals advance rare earth supply chain diversification away from Chinese sources [6]
- Labor shortages are prompting structural adaptation across industries, including foreign worker training at JR East and autonomous vehicle partnerships [6]
- Wage growth lagging inflation and a deepening loneliness epidemic signal household-level stress beneath headline economic improvements [7]
