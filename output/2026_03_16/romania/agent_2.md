Romania's economy faced significant challenges in the automotive sector, as Dacia announced major workforce reductions planned for 2026. The Sindicatul Autoturisme Dacia reported that 1,200 jobs will be eliminated at the Mioveni plant due to robotization and difficult economic conditions [5]. The union attributed these cuts to inadequate infrastructure development, citing the unfulfilled promise of the Pitești-Sibiu highway, high energy costs, and fiscal instability that have made Romania less competitive compared to other production centers [5].

Production decisions further illustrated Romania's declining attractiveness for automotive manufacturing. The Dacia Striker model will be manufactured in Turkey, while a new electric vehicle from segment A will be produced in Slovenia rather than at Mioveni [5]. These relocations reflect broader competitiveness issues, as the union noted that Romania's dependence on exports—which account for over 90% of Dacia's production—makes the facility particularly vulnerable to infrastructure and fiscal policy shortcomings [5].

The economic impact extends beyond direct employment, with the Dacia plant supporting approximately 10,000 direct jobs and tens of thousands of indirect positions through supplier networks [5]. Automobile Dacia initiated a voluntary departure campaign in March, offering substantial compensation packages based on employee tenure, as part of workforce adjustment preparations [5]. Despite operating at maximum capacity and producing approximately 1,300 vehicles daily, the facility faces pressure from inflation, rising energy costs, and high Romanian taxation levels [5].
