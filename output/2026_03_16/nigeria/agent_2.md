Nigeria's cultural landscape reflects the diversity of its more than 250 ethnic groups, who speak over 500 different languages and maintain distinct artistic traditions [3]. The country serves as the fashion, technological and creative hub in Africa, with its film industry ranking as the second largest globally [3]. Traditional crafts continue alongside contemporary expressions, with the Kofar Mata Dye Pit in Kano, established in 1498, representing Africa's oldest dye pit and preserving centuries-old textile production methods [3].

The nation's artistic heritage spans from historical Benin Kingdom works to modern expressions by contemporary artists including Nike Davis-Okundaye, Ben Enwonwu, Bruce Onobrakpeya and Erhabor Ogieva Emokpae [3]. Nigeria achieved literary recognition when playwright Wole Soyinka became Africa's first Nobel Laureate in Literature in 1986 [3]. Two UNESCO World Heritage Sites—the Sukur Cultural Landscape in Adamawa and Osun-Osogbo Sacred Grove in Osun—preserve significant cultural and historical sites [3]. The country's biodiversity supports over 1,000 documented butterfly species, with new species still being discovered [3].

Nigeria operates as a federal republic with power distributed between the federal government, 36 states, and the Federal Capital Territory [2]. The government structure includes an executive branch headed by the President, currently Bola Ahmed Tinubu who assumed office on May 29, 2023, serving as the 16th President after previously governing Lagos State from 1999 to 2007 [2]. The legislative branch comprises the Senate, led by Senate President Godswill Akpabio, and the House of Representatives, headed by Speaker Tajudeen Abbas [2].

The country's economy draws from its diverse geography, which spans approximately 923,769 square kilometers and includes distinct ecological zones supporting different economic activities [1]. The northern savanna region supports cattle herding and grain cultivation, while the Jos Plateau contains significant mineral resources including tin deposits [1]. The Guinea Savanna belt serves as the agricultural backbone, with yam, cassava, and maize cultivation dominating the landscape [1]. The Niger Delta region, characterized by mangrove forests and lagoons, constitutes the center of Nigeria's oil industry, though this has created environmental challenges including oil spills and land degradation [1].
