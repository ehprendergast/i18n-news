# Indonesia: International Briefing

*Report date: December 19, 2024*

Indonesia's political landscape underwent fundamental transformation following the Indonesian Revolution of 1998, which restored democracy after decades under independence leader Sukarno and military dictator Suharto [5]. The archipelagic nation operates as a republic with 34 administrative provinces distributed across its territory [7]. Recent political tensions have emerged over proposed changes to election law, resulting in police clashes with protesters as leaders sought to reverse a constitutional court decision that would have opened elections to their rivals [6]. A new leader was recently sworn in with the largest cabinet in decades, which observers characterise as a political reward to predecessor Joko Widodo [6]. The outgoing administration's signature legacy project, the planned new capital city Nusantara, has taken on symbolic importance, with Indonesia celebrating Independence Day in the future capital [6].

Indonesia maintains Southeast Asia's largest economy and ranks 17th globally by nominal GDP [7]. The country leads global palm oil production and holds second place worldwide in rubber, coffee, and tin production [7]. Major economic sectors encompass manufacturing, agriculture, and services, reflecting the diversified nature of the national economy [7]. The nation's infrastructure includes 297 airports with major international hubs in Jakarta and Bali, 300 ports connecting the archipelago, and the Trans-Java toll road network spanning 1,167 kilometres [7].

Indonesian society comprises approximately 1,300 ethnic groups speaking over 700 languages across a population of 282.5 million inhabitants, making it the fourth most populous country globally and the most populous Muslim-majority nation [7][5]. The national language is Bahasa Indonesia [5]. Jakarta serves as the capital with 10.5 million metropolitan inhabitants, while other major cities include Surabaya, Bandung, Medan, and Semarang [7]. The country's geography spans over 17,500 islands, with its position on the Pacific Ring of Fire creating frequent seismic activity [1][7].

Cultural norms emphasise indirect communication, with Indonesians rarely disagreeing in public and seldom saying 'no', instead using 'Belum' meaning 'not yet' [2]. Traditional family structures have evolved significantly, with Indonesians historically maintaining large families but now typically limiting themselves to two children [2]. Social visiting patterns include unannounced calls with established protocols, while dating practices vary between urban and rural areas, with Western-style courtship more prevalent in cities though arranged marriages have largely given way to individual choice [2]. Indonesians prefer modest dress whether wearing Western or traditional styles [2].

Traditional sports remain culturally significant, particularly sepak takraw, where two teams attempt to keep a rattan ball airborne using only their feet [2]. Rice forms the dietary foundation, typically served with vegetables, fish, and hot sauces, accompanied by tea [2]. The country attracts millions of international visitors annually to destinations including Bali's beaches, the Borobudur Temple Complex, Komodo National Park, Raja Ampat's coral reefs, and the Mount Bromo volcanic complex [7].

Recent international developments include Pope Francis completing a historic visit to Indonesia focusing on inter-religious harmony [6]. A New Zealand pilot held hostage by separatists in West Papua was freed after an extended captivity [6]. Social challenges persist including poverty, illiteracy, political instability, and regional separatism [5].

## Key Developments

• Democracy restored following 1998 Indonesian Revolution, ending decades of authoritarian rule under Sukarno and Suharto
• New leadership installed with largest cabinet in decades, interpreted as political accommodation with previous administration
• Constitutional tensions over electoral law changes resulted in street protests and police confrontations
• Economy maintains position as Southeast Asia's largest, ranking 17th globally with leadership in palm oil production
• Population of 282.5 million across 1,300 ethnic groups makes Indonesia the world's most populous Muslim-majority nation
• Planned new capital Nusantara proceeds as major infrastructure legacy project of outgoing administration
• Pope Francis completed historic visit emphasising inter-religious harmony across diverse archipelagic society
• West Papua separatist incident resolved with release of New Zealand pilot after extended hostage situation
