# Cyprus: International Briefing

*Report date: December 19, 2024*

The Republic of Cyprus operates as a divided island nation with the internationally recognised government controlling the southern two-thirds whilst the self-proclaimed Turkish Republic of Northern Cyprus administers the northern territory [3]. The division stems from events in 1974, when Greek Cypriots attempted to annex the island to Greece, prompting Turkish military intervention [3]. The Republic of Cyprus maintains that entry to the country must not occur through the northern territory [3]. Cyprus is administratively divided into six districts: Famagusta, Kyrenia, Larnaca, Limassol, Nicosia and Paphos [1].

Nicosia serves as the administrative capital and primary financial centre [1]. The island's strategic economic positioning in the northeastern Mediterranean Sea places it at the crossroads of Europe, Asia, and Africa [4]. Cyprus lies approximately 40 miles south of Turkey, 60 miles west of Syria, and 480 miles southeast of mainland Greece [4]. The Turkish lira serves as currency in the northern region [3].

Cyprus ranks as the third largest Mediterranean island after Sicily and Sardinia with an area of 9,251 square kilometres [1]. The island is also the third most populous Mediterranean island [1]. Mount Olympus stands as the island's highest point at 1,952 metres [1]. The Kyrenia Mountains reach a maximum elevation of 1,024 metres [1]. The Pedieos River flows approximately 100 kilometres from the Troodos Mountains through Nicosia to Famagusta Bay [1]. Nicosia is situated in the Mesaoria Plains along the Pedieos River [1].

The island features over 100 dams and reservoirs as principal water sources [1]. Cyprus receives up to 3,000 hours of sunshine annually [3]. Notable beaches include Nissi Beach near Ayia Napa, Fig Tree Bay in Protaras, and Coral Bay [3]. Many coastal areas receive Blue Flag international recognition [3].

Cypriot cuisine demonstrates close relations to Greek culinary traditions [2]. Traditional costumes appear at folk festivals and special events [2]. The island's cultural identity reflects its position as a bridge between different civilisational influences due to its Mediterranean crossroads location [4].

Football serves as the primary spectator and participation sport across Cyprus [2]. Mount Olympus operates three ski runs during winter season from January [2]. These recreational offerings reflect the island's diverse topographical features, from coastal areas to mountainous terrain.

## Key Developments

• Cyprus remains politically divided since 1974 between the Republic of Cyprus controlling the southern two-thirds and the Turkish Republic of Northern Cyprus administering the northern territory
• The island occupies a strategic position in the northeastern Mediterranean at the crossroads of Europe, Asia, and Africa, approximately 40 miles south of Turkey
• Cyprus ranks as the third largest and third most populous Mediterranean island with an area of 9,251 square kilometres
• Nicosia functions as both the administrative capital and primary financial centre, situated in the Mesaoria Plains
• The island receives up to 3,000 hours of annual sunshine with many beaches achieving Blue Flag international recognition
• Water infrastructure comprises over 100 dams and reservoirs serving as principal water sources
• Football dominates as the primary sport while Mount Olympus offers skiing opportunities during winter months
• Cultural traditions maintain close ties to Greek customs while reflecting the island's position as a Mediterranean crossroads
