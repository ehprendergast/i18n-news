# Egypt: International Briefing

*Report date: January 22, 2026*

Egypt's government has implemented emergency energy conservation measures as the country grapples with mounting fuel costs linked to regional conflict. Prime Minister Mostafa Madbouly announced that retail establishments, restaurants, and cafes must close by 21:00 daily, while government offices will close at 18:00 [20][21][22]. The administration has also mandated remote work every Sunday in April for public and private sector employees, excluding essential services and manufacturing [22]. These measures reflect the government's attempt to manage an acute energy crisis that threatens economic stability.

The energy crisis stems from Egypt's structural dependence on imported fuel, with the country's monthly natural gas bill rising from $560 million to $1.65 billion since the Iran conflict began [20]. This represents an increase of over $1.1 billion in monthly expenditure, placing severe strain on government finances. Despite continuing to export natural gas, Egypt's domestic production has declined, forcing increased reliance on imports [20]. The situation has been complicated by Egypt's gas import agreement with Israel signed in December 2025, as disruptions at Israel's Leviathan offshore gas field have affected supply arrangements [20].

Egypt's electricity generation system relies heavily on natural gas, with over 80 percent of the country's power produced from this source [20]. Supply chain disruptions have therefore significantly impacted the national power grid, forcing authorities to implement rolling conservation measures. Street lighting has been dimmed by up to 50 percent in some areas, while fuel allocations for state vehicles have been reduced by 30 percent [20][22]. The government raised fuel prices by three Egyptian pounds per liter in March, citing higher import and logistics costs [20].

The tourism sector has been exempted from energy restrictions due to its economic importance, representing approximately 10 percent of Egypt's economy [21]. Hotels including Marriott and Cosmopolitan have acquired generators to maintain operations despite broader energy conservation requirements [21]. This exemption reflects the government's recognition of tourism's critical role in foreign currency earnings and employment generation.

Egypt joined seven other Arab and Islamic nations in condemning Israeli legislation authorizing death penalties for Palestinian prisoners in the occupied West Bank [8]. Foreign ministers from Jordan, Indonesia, Pakistan, Turkey, Saudi Arabia, Qatar, Egypt, and the UAE characterized the discriminatory application of capital punishment as a dangerous escalation that could inflame regional tensions [8]. The ministers expressed concern over reports of systematic violations against Palestinian prisoners, including torture, inhumane treatment, and denial of basic rights [8].
