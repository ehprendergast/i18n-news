Japan's political structure is mentioned only in passing, noting that the country is organized into 8 regions and 47 prefectures [3].

Japan's geography encompasses several thousand islands, with Honshu, Hokkaido, Kyushu and Shikoku representing the four largest landmasses [3]. The archipelago's total area corresponds roughly to that of Germany, with more than 50 percent consisting of mountainous terrain covered by forests [3]. The population stands at approximately 125 million, including around 3 million foreign residents [3]. The country's location at the convergence of several continental plates results in frequent seismic activity and numerous volcanoes, with Mount Fuji serving as both the nation's most famous volcano and highest mountain [3].

The climate varies considerably across Japan's north-south expanse [3]. Major cities including Tokyo experience temperate to subtropical conditions with four distinct seasons, characterized by mild winters and hot, humid summers [3]. A rainy season occurs in early summer, while typhoons regularly affect parts of the country during late summer months [3]. Hokkaido and the Sea of Japan coast experience colder temperatures with substantial snowfall, while Okinawa maintains warmer conditions, with January temperatures averaging 17 degrees Celsius [3].

Osaka serves as Japan's second largest metropolitan area and has functioned as the Kansai Region's economic center for centuries [8]. The city was previously known as Naniwa and briefly served as Japan's capital before the Nara Period [8]. In the 16th century, Toyotomi Hideyoshi selected Osaka as the location for his castle, and the city might have become Japan's political capital had Tokugawa Ieyasu not eliminated the Toyotomi lineage and established his government in Edo [8]. Fukuoka ranks among Japan's ten most populated cities and, due to its proximity to the Asian mainland, has served as an important harbor city for many centuries [6]. The current city of Fukuoka resulted from the 1889 merger of the port city of Hakata and the former castle town of Fukuoka [6].
