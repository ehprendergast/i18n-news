Nigeria's geographic position in West Africa encompasses approximately 923,769 square kilometers, with borders extending to Benin, Chad, Cameroon, and Niger, while maintaining an 853-kilometer coastline along the Gulf of Guinea [1]. The country's terrain varies from northern savanna regions suitable for cattle herding and grain cultivation to the semi-arid Sahel zone, which experiences sparse rainfall and supports drought-resistant crops and pastoralism [1]. Central highlands, including the Jos Plateau reaching 1,295 meters in elevation, contain significant mineral resources with tin mining as a major activity [1]. The southern regions transition through Guinea Savanna, where yam, cassava, and maize cultivation dominates the agricultural landscape, to rainforest belts that have experienced extensive deforestation due to logging and agricultural conversion [1].

The Niger Delta region, characterized by mangrove forests, swamps, and lagoons, serves as the heart of Nigeria's oil industry, though this has resulted in environmental challenges including oil spills and land degradation [1]. The northern savanna's grasslands and scattered trees provide ideal conditions for herding and grain production, while the region's transition into the Sahel creates challenges for agricultural productivity due to unpredictable rainfall patterns [1]. The Jos Plateau's volcanic soils and mild climate enable diverse crop cultivation including vegetables, fruits, and tubers, while its unique rock formations contribute to the area's geological significance [1]. The eastern section contains Chappal Waddi, Nigeria's highest peak at 2,419 meters, located within the Guinea Savanna belt [1].

Nigeria's agricultural economy relies heavily on the Guinea Savanna region, which experiences greater rainfall than northern areas, enabling taller grass growth and more densely populated tree coverage [1]. The rainforest belt, despite deforestation pressures, continues providing timber and non-timber forest products while maintaining high humidity and lush vegetation including various hardwood species [1]. The Niger River, as the country's most significant waterway, drains through the densely populated delta region into the Atlantic Ocean, creating a complex ecosystem of waterways and tributaries [1].

Soccer maintains its position as Nigeria's most popular sport, while wrestling, archery, horse racing, basketball, and polo attract significant followings [2]. Nigerian families generally operate under male-dominated structures, though specific details vary among ethnic groups [2]. The national diet frequently incorporates cassava, yams, and rice, with Nigerians demonstrating preference for hot, spicy foods that reflect climatic conditions [2]. Conservative dress remains standard among Muslim populations for both men and women, while non-Muslim eastern regions adopt more casual approaches to personal appearance [2]. Various ethnic groups maintain unique eating customs, with some populations eating with the right hand only while others utilize utensils [2].
