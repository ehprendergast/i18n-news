Myanmar continues to face deepening political instability five years after the military coup, with the United Nations reporting escalating violence, mass displacement, and widespread violations of international law [4]. The military-controlled elections concluded in January 2026 have further entrenched repression rather than restored civilian rule, according to UN officials [4]. The three-phased electoral process was conducted in only 263 of 330 townships, largely confined to urban areas under military control, while excluding conflict-affected regions, displaced populations, and minorities including the Rohingya [4]. The main opposition National League for Democracy, which won the 2020 elections decisively, was barred from participating alongside dozens of other parties opposed to military rule [4]. Military forces conducted 408 air attacks during the election period, killing at least 170 civilians, including a January 22 airstrike in Bhamo Township, Kachin State, that reportedly killed up to 50 civilians [4]. To suppress dissent, authorities arrested 324 men and 80 women under an election protection law, with one case resulting in a 49-year prison sentence for posting anti-election material [4].

The humanitarian and economic situation has deteriorated significantly, with nearly 5.2 million people displaced both within the country and across borders [4]. The UN reports acute food insecurity affecting the population, while the Secretary-General expressed deep concern about rapidly deteriorating conditions and their regional repercussions, including rising transnational crime and economic volatility [4]. Military airstrikes continue to target civilian areas, with resistance forces conducting operations to rescue civilian hostages in occupied territories, as documented in Bago Region [5]. In Nattalin Township, Bago, regime forces killed three people and took at least five civilians hostage while torching hundreds of houses [5].

Myanmar's education system remains in crisis five years after the coup, with a steep decline in students taking the annual matriculation examination indicating systemic collapse [5]. The country's social fabric continues to face severe strain as violence persists across multiple regions, affecting basic services and civil society functions.

Myanmar's cultural heritage encompasses over 100 officially recognized ethnic groups, with Buddhism playing a central role in national identity alongside Christianity, Islam, Hinduism, and traditional beliefs [6]. The nation serves as a cultural crossroads between South Asia and mainland Southeast Asia, positioned between India, China, Thailand, Bangladesh, and Laos [6]. The Republic of the Union of Myanmar has a population exceeding 51 million, with major racial groups including Bamar, Kachin, Kayah, Kayin, Chin, Mon, Rakhine, and Shan [7]. The country's geography spans 676,578 square kilometers, featuring tropical rainforests, mountain ranges, and river valleys dominated by the Irrawaddy River system [8]. The Himalayan range extends into northern Myanmar, with Hkakabo Razi representing the country's highest point at 19,296 feet [8].
