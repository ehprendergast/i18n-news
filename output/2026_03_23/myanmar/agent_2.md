Myanmar's cultural landscape encompasses significant geographical and ethnic diversity. The country is home to more than 100 officially recognized ethnic groups, with major groups including Bamar, Kachin, Kayah, Kayin, Chin, Mon, Rakhine and Shan [7]. Buddhism plays a central role in national identity, though Christianity, Islam, Hinduism, and traditional beliefs also form part of the country's religious landscape [6]. The Republic of the Union of Myanmar maintains what officials describe as the biggest cultural heritage in Southeast Asia, with a Burmese calendar populated by "12 seasonal festivals" born from religious, social, and political traditions [7]. The country's cultural sites include the ancient temples of Bagan, floating villages of Inle Lake, and urban centers in Yangon [6].

Economic and commercial infrastructure faces severe disruption. The United Nations reports that Myanmar's crisis has deepened five years after the military coup, marked by economic volatility and soaring humanitarian needs [4]. The country's major rivers, including the Ayeyarwady (Irrawaddy), Chindwin, and Salween, historically shaped trade, agriculture, and settlement patterns, with agriculture, natural resources, energy, border trade, and tourism remaining key economic sectors [6]. Myanmar's geography spans 676,578 square kilometers and borders India, Bangladesh, China, Thailand, and Laos, providing access to global trade through coastlines along the Bay of Bengal and Andaman Sea [8].

Political conditions have deteriorated significantly since the military seized power five years ago. The United Nations reports widespread violations of international human rights law and international humanitarian law, with a cycle of impunity persisting [4]. Three-phased elections imposed by the military concluded recently, but UN officials state these have further polarized society and intensified violence rather than providing a credible political pathway [4]. The elections were held in only 263 of 330 townships, largely confined to urban areas under military control, with large conflict-affected regions excluded along with displaced populations and minorities, including the Rohingya [4]. The main opposition National League for Democracy, which won the 2020 elections, was barred from participating, and dozens of other parties opposed to military rule were banned [4]. During the voting period between December 2025 and January 2026, open sources documented 408 military air attacks that killed at least 170 civilians [4]. The military arrested 324 men and 80 women under an election protection law, including for minor online activity, with one case resulting in a 49-year prison sentence for posting anti-election material [4]. Nearly 5.2 million people are displaced both inside the country and across borders, with acute food insecurity reported [4].
