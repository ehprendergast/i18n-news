Finland: International Briefing

Report date: 10 March 2026

Finland's right-wing coalition government has proposed amendments to the Nuclear Energy Act and Criminal Code that would remove a blanket legislative ban on nuclear weapons dating to the 1980s [1,2,3]. The proposed changes would permit nuclear weapons within Finnish territory for the purposes of homeland defence, NATO collective defence, and broader defence cooperation, while leaving the manufacture and detonation of nuclear weapons as criminal offences [3]. Defence Minister Häkkänen argued that the existing prohibition "does not meet the needs that Finland has as a NATO member" [1], and President Stubb framed the revision as ensuring there are "no law-based obstacles hampering the defence and deterrence by NATO" [2]. The government has stated explicitly that it does not seek to host nuclear weapons and that NATO has made no such deployment plans [2].

Finland joined NATO in 2023 following Russia's invasion of Ukraine, sharing a border of more than 1,000 kilometres with Russia [1,3]. The legislative proposal is presented against that strategic backdrop: most NATO members carry no equivalent statutory restrictions, and Helsinki argues that a unilateral legal prohibition places Finland in an anomalous position within the alliance [3]. Whether the amendment would ever translate into actual weapons hosting remains an open and contested political question.

The ruling coalition commands a parliamentary majority sufficient to advance the legislation [3], but the proposal has drawn sharp opposition from the Social Democrats (SDP), the Green League, and the Left Alliance [2]. SDP chair Lindtman argued the move would distance Finland from established Nordic nuclear policies [2], and senior SDP figure Tuppurainen contended the amendment goes further than any equivalent step taken by Finland's Nordic neighbours [3]. Critics also noted that opposition leaders were informed only one day in advance of the announcement [2]. Draft amendments are currently in public consultation, with comments due by 2 April [2].

A comparative dimension runs through the domestic debate. Sweden, Denmark, and Norway all maintain positions against hosting nuclear weapons, but through political declarations rather than binding legislation [2,3]. Erästö of SIPRI has noted that Finland is the only Nordic country with a full legal ban; its neighbours rely on political commitments [2]. The proposed Finnish shift would therefore represent a structural departure from Nordic practice, even if the practical outcome — no nuclear weapons on Finnish soil — were to remain identical in the near term. Peace Union representative Lodenius argued the change would not make Finland safer [2].

On the diplomatic front, Prime Minister Orpo is scheduled to meet his Canadian and Nordic counterparts in Norway [4]. President Stubb recently concluded a state visit to India, during which Finland-India relations were elevated to a Strategic Partnership; Stubb and Prime Minister Modi jointly inaugurated the 11th Raisina Dialogue [5]. Separately, NATO AWACS aircraft conducted their first airborne surveillance mission in Finnish airspace [5], a practical illustration of alliance integration proceeding in parallel with the legislative debate over nuclear policy.

The government has submitted its budget proposal for 2026 [4]. In the energy sector, Helsinki Energy Group has announced plans to invest in small modular nuclear reactors [5] — a development that sits alongside, though is legally and technically distinct from, the nuclear weapons debate. Faron Pharmaceuticals confirmed a €40 million investment round to fund a blood cancer clinical trial [5], and the Finnish aviation sector is recording gradual recovery in passenger volumes [5]. The Helsinki Regional Transport Authority (HSL) has announced potential workforce reductions [5].

Social data published by YLE presents a more troubled picture of Finnish society's lower rungs. More than one-third of adults under 29 were assessed as at risk of poverty or social marginalisation in 2024, representing an increase of approximately 56,000 people compared with 2023 and a rise of nearly ten percentage points since 2018 [7]. In absolute terms, 958,000 people — out of a total population of 5.66 million — were identified as at risk [7]. Among under-35s living alone, 54% fall into this category [7]. The trend is uneven across age cohorts: risk rose among the 30-to-49 group while declining among older adults and seniors [7].

Finland retained its ranking as the world's happiest country [6]. Professor Martela attributed the standing to the Nordic welfare model [6]. An Aalto University study tracking 40,000 children found that extending pre-school from one to two years produced no measurable improvement in school-age academic skills [6], a finding with potential implications for education policy discussions. Finnish cities have been identified as European leaders in urban tree canopy cover [6], and the Laboratory of Hope exhibition runs at Aalto University through 27 March 2026 [6].

Cultural activity extended across several fronts. Artist Marjo Smolander's exhibition "Cosmologies," a Finnish-African collaboration, drew attention [5], and between 15,000 and 20,000 people marched in Helsinki for International Women's Day [5]. Jaakko Torvinen's Puusauna design won Wallpaper Magazine's Life Enhancer of the Year Award [6], and more than 720,000 Finns — approximately one in eight — are regular cold-water swimmers [6]. A Finnish pair won the UK Wife Carrying Race [5].
