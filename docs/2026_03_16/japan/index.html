Japan: International Briefing

Report date: December 19, 2024

The Japanese government has finalized plans to significantly tighten permanent residency requirements beginning April 2027 [10]. This policy shift represents a substantial change in the country's immigration framework, affecting the pathways for long-term foreign residents to secure permanent status. The timing of these new requirements suggests a coordinated approach to managing immigration flows ahead of demographic challenges.

Alongside the residency requirement changes, immigration fees are set to rise substantially, with costs increasing by 10-20 times current levels [12]. These dramatic fee increases will fundamentally alter the financial burden of immigration processes in Japan. The scale of the increases indicates a potential shift toward limiting immigration through economic barriers rather than administrative restrictions alone.

Japan's economic landscape faces significant headwinds, particularly in the automotive sector. Honda Motor expects a net loss of up to 690 billion yen due to declining electric vehicle demand in North America [11]. This substantial loss projection reflects broader challenges facing Japanese automakers as they navigate shifting global demand patterns for electric vehicles. The company's difficulties highlight the risks associated with international market dependencies in Japan's export-oriented economy.

Transportation costs have increased markedly across the country's rail network. East Japan Railways raised fares by an average of 7.1 percent, ending a 39-year fare freeze [11,12]. This fare increase represents the first adjustment in nearly four decades and signals a departure from long-standing pricing policies that had maintained stable transportation costs for commuters and travelers.

Japan's demographic composition remains relatively stable, with the country maintaining a population of approximately 125 million, including around 3 million foreign residents [7]. However, the nation continues to face ongoing demographic challenges with its aging population [13]. These demographic pressures provide context for the government's immigration policy adjustments and economic planning considerations.

The country's geography encompasses several thousand islands, with Honshu, Hokkaido, Kyushu and Shikoku being the four largest [7]. This island geography continues to influence infrastructure development and transportation planning across the archipelago.

Criminal activity has captured public attention with the arrest of seven men, including suspected yakuza members, for a robbery of approximately 423 million yen in Tokyo [10,15]. The scale of this theft and the involvement of organized crime figures reflects ongoing challenges with criminal enterprises in major urban areas.

Infrastructure incidents have disrupted daily life in major cities. A large metal pipe approximately 3.6 meters in diameter protruded from underground in Osaka's Umeda district [10,15]. The pipe initially rose 13 meters above ground before settling back to 1.6 meters [10], causing significant disruption in one of the city's busiest commercial areas.

Spring seasonal patterns showed early developments this year, with cherry blossoms beginning to bloom in Kochi and Gifu prefectures in March 2026 [10,11,12]. The Somei-Yoshino variety bloomed seven days earlier than the previous year in Kochi [10], and cherry blossoms were expected to bloom a few days ahead of average timing across the country in 2026 [5].

Japanese motorsport achieved international success as Toyota driver Takamoto Katsuta secured his first World Rally Championship victory at the Kenya Safari Rally [10]. This victory represents a significant achievement for Japanese motorsport on the global stage.
