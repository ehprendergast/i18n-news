Philippines: International Briefing

Report date: December 19, 2024

The Philippines operates under a constitution established following independence in 1946, after more than three centuries of Spanish colonial rule [8]. The current president, Ferdinand Marcos Jr., son of the former authoritarian president, won a landslide victory in May 2022 and enlisted Sara Duterte as vice-president, daughter of outgoing President Rodrigo Duterte, thereby uniting two populist right-wing dynasties [8]. Duterte had gained power in 2016 with promises of aggressive campaigns against crime, drugs, and corruption [8]. Filipino and English serve as official languages alongside regional dialects [8].

President Marcos Jr. recently declared a national energy emergency in response to Middle East conflicts threatening the country's energy supply [9]. The emergency declaration grants government authority to control fuel prices and expedite imports from alternative suppliers, including Russia [9]. Philippine authorities report maintaining fuel reserves sufficient for approximately 45 days at normal consumption levels [9]. The closure of the Strait of Hormuz has disrupted approximately 20% of global crude oil supply, totaling 20 million barrels daily, with about 80% of oil through this waterway bound for Asian markets [9]. The International Energy Agency has characterised the current crisis as exceeding the combined impact of 1970s energy shocks [9].

The Philippine archipelago comprises over 7,600 islands positioned in Southeast Asia below Taiwan, east of Vietnam, and north of Indonesia, spanning 300,000 square kilometres [8][10]. The population of 109 million is concentrated primarily on 11 main islands, with Manila serving as the capital [8]. The country experiences frequent natural disasters from approximately 20 active volcanoes, alongside regular typhoons and storms [8][12][13].

Filipino society reflects diverse historical influences, beginning with Austronesian seafarers and later incorporating Indian cultural traits from the 14th century and Islam establishment in the Sulu Archipelago during the 15th century [8]. The Laguna Copperplate Inscription from 900 AD represents the earliest record of a Philippine language [8]. The average Filipino family typically has four or more children [7][8]. Rice constitutes the primary staple food prepared through various methods [7].

Cultural traditions emphasise hospitality, with customs focused on making guests comfortable while expecting tactful reciprocation [7]. Teenagers form peer groups called barkadas, which serve as essential social units in Filipino youth culture [7]. These social structures reflect the broader importance of interpersonal relationships in Philippine society.

The media landscape features commercial interests controlling significant portions of television, radio, and print outlets [8]. Television is dominated by free-to-air networks ABS-CBN and GMA [8]. Despite constitutional press freedom guarantees, the Philippines ranks among the world's most dangerous countries for journalists [8].

Regional neighbours have implemented similar energy conservation measures, with South Korea launching energy-saving campaigns, Japan releasing emergency reserves, and Thailand and Vietnam requesting energy conservation [9]. These coordinated responses reflect the broader impact of supply chain disruptions across Asian markets dependent on Middle Eastern oil imports.

The archipelago's strategic location has historically positioned it as a trading crossroads, facilitating cultural integration from various Asian influences over centuries [10]. Archaeological evidence demonstrates early written language use and cultural development predating European contact [8]. This geographic positioning continues to influence contemporary economic and political relationships.

The country's current energy crisis represents part of a broader regional challenge affecting multiple Asian economies dependent on oil imports through traditional shipping routes [9]. The government's emergency measures reflect immediate responses to supply disruptions while authorities explore alternative supplier arrangements.

The Philippines faces ongoing challenges balancing press freedoms with media safety concerns, managing natural disaster risks across its volcanic and typhoon-prone geography, and addressing energy security vulnerabilities exposed by regional conflicts [8][9]. These issues intersect with broader questions of economic resilience and political stability in the archipelago nation.
