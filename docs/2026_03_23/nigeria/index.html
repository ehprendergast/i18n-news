Nigeria: International Briefing

Report date: December 19, 2024

Nigeria occupies a strategic position in West Africa, covering approximately 923,769 square kilometers with borders extending to Benin, Chad, Cameroon, and Niger [1]. The country maintains an 853-kilometer coastline along the Gulf of Guinea, providing crucial access to Atlantic maritime trade routes [1]. This geographic positioning has shaped Nigeria's role as a regional hub for both continental and international commerce.

The Nigerian economy demonstrates significant regional specialization across distinct ecological zones. The northern savanna regions support extensive cattle herding and grain cultivation, transitioning into the semi-arid Sahel zone where sparse rainfall limits agricultural activities to drought-resistant crops and pastoralism [1]. The Guinea Savanna region dominates national agricultural production through cultivation of yam, cassava, and maize [1]. The Niger Delta region serves as the center of Nigeria's oil industry, though this concentration of petroleum extraction has resulted in substantial environmental challenges including oil spills and land degradation [1].

Mining operations contribute to the economic landscape, particularly in the central Jos Plateau region, which reaches 1,295 meters in elevation and hosts significant tin mining activities [1]. The plateau's volcanic soils and mild climate also support diverse crop production, demonstrating the area's dual economic function [1]. Chappal Waddi, Nigeria's highest peak at 2,419 meters, represents the country's most elevated terrain [1].

Environmental pressures affect multiple regions. The southern rainforest belt has experienced extensive deforestation due to logging and agricultural conversion [1]. The Niger Delta, characterized by mangrove forests, swamps, and lagoons, faces ongoing degradation from industrial activities [1]. These environmental changes reflect the tensions between economic development and ecological preservation.

Nigerian society operates through predominantly male-dominated family structures, though specific arrangements vary among the country's diverse ethnic groups [2]. Cultural practices demonstrate significant regional variation, with conservative dress codes standard among Muslim populations while non-Muslim eastern regions adopt more casual approaches to attire [2]. Marriage practices commonly involve bridal token or dowry payments across different communities [2].

Dietary patterns center on staple crops including cassava, yams, and rice, with Nigerians demonstrating strong preferences for hot, spicy food preparations [2]. Eating customs vary by ethnic group, with some populations eating with the right hand only while others utilize utensils [2]. Gift-giving during visits is appreciated though not mandatory in Nigerian social interactions [2].

Soccer maintains its position as Nigeria's most popular sport, attracting widespread participation and spectatorship [2]. Wrestling, archery, horse racing, basketball, and polo also command significant followings, reflecting the country's diverse recreational preferences [2]. These sporting activities span both traditional and modern forms of entertainment.

Law enforcement cooperation has expanded to address international criminal activities, with bilateral efforts targeting commercial fraud operations [2]. Despite these measures, sophisticated confidence schemes continue to target international victims through internet platforms, representing an ongoing challenge for Nigerian authorities [2].

The country's agricultural foundation remains tied to climatic and geographical conditions. The Sahel zone's semi-arid environment supports pastoralism and specialized farming techniques adapted to limited rainfall [1]. The Jos Plateau's unique elevation and soil conditions enable production of vegetables, fruits, and tubers distinct from other regions [1].

Regional economic activities reflect Nigeria's diverse geographical zones, from the petroleum-focused Niger Delta to the agricultural Guinea Savanna belt and the mineral-rich Jos Plateau [1]. This geographic diversity provides multiple economic foundations while creating distinct regional challenges and opportunities across the national territory.

Key Developments

• Nigeria's economy demonstrates clear regional specialization, with northern areas focused on pastoralism and grain production, central regions supporting mining and diverse agriculture, and southern areas dominated by oil extraction

• The Niger Delta faces mounting environmental challenges from petroleum industry operations, including oil spills and land degradation affecting the region's mangrove ecosystems

• Agricultural production remains concentrated in the Guinea Savanna region, which benefits from favorable rainfall patterns for yam, cassava, and maize cultivation

• Soccer dominates Nigeria's sporting landscape while traditional activities including wrestling, archery, and horse racing maintain strong cultural significance

• Family structures across ethnic groups remain predominantly male-dominated, with marriage practices commonly involving dowry or bridal token payments

• Internet-based fraud schemes continue to evolve despite increased bilateral law enforcement cooperation targeting commercial fraud operations

• The Jos Plateau region maintains dual economic importance through both tin mining operations and specialized agricultural production enabled by its elevated climate and volcanic soils
