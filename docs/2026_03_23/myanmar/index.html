Myanmar: International Briefing

Report date: December 19, 2024

Myanmar's political crisis has intensified five years after the military coup, with the United Nations reporting deepening instability and widespread violations of international law [4]. The military-controlled elections that concluded in January 2026 have further entrenched authoritarian rule rather than providing any credible pathway to civilian governance [4]. These three-phased elections were conducted in only 263 of Myanmar's 330 townships, largely confined to urban areas under military control while systematically excluding conflict-affected regions and displaced populations [4].

The electoral process deliberately barred the main opposition National League for Democracy, which had won the 2020 elections decisively, alongside dozens of other parties opposed to military rule [4]. The elections excluded minority populations, including the Rohingya, reinforcing patterns of systematic disenfranchisement [4]. During the voting period, military forces conducted 408 documented air attacks that killed at least 170 civilians, including a January 22 airstrike in Bhamo Township, Kachin State, that reportedly killed up to 50 civilians [4].

State repression has intensified through legal mechanisms designed to suppress dissent. Authorities arrested 404 people under election protection laws, including 324 men and 80 women, with prosecutions targeting even minor online activity [4]. One case resulted in a 49-year prison sentence for posting anti-election material, illustrating the severity of restrictions on political expression [4]. Military forces continue conducting operations against civilian areas, with documented cases in Nattalin Township, Bago, where regime forces killed three people and took at least five civilians hostage while torching hundreds of houses [5].

The humanitarian and economic situation has deteriorated dramatically, with nearly 5.2 million people displaced both within the country and across international borders [4]. The United Nations reports acute food insecurity affecting the population, while economic volatility continues to undermine basic services and livelihoods [4]. Myanmar's education system shows steep decline in matriculation exam participation, indicating systemic institutional collapse five years after the coup [5]. Resistance operations continue in occupied territories, including documented civilian hostage rescues in Bago Region [5].

Myanmar's diverse social fabric encompasses over 100 officially recognized ethnic groups across a population exceeding 51 million [6][7]. Major ethnic groups include Bamar, Kachin, Kayah, Kayin, Chin, Mon, Rakhine and Shan, reflecting the country's position as a cultural crossroads between South Asia and mainland Southeast Asia [7]. Buddhism plays a central role in national identity, though Christianity, Islam, Hinduism, and traditional beliefs also form part of the religious landscape [6].

The country maintains what officials describe as the biggest cultural heritage in Southeast Asia, with a traditional calendar featuring 12 seasonal festivals derived from religious, social, and political traditions [7]. Myanmar's cultural sites include the ancient temples of Bagan, floating villages of Inle Lake, and urban centers in Yangon, representing centuries of accumulated heritage [6]. This cultural richness exists within a geographic area spanning 676,578 square kilometers, bordered by India, Bangladesh, China, Thailand, and Laos [8].

Myanmar's geography features diverse terrain from tropical rainforests to mountain ranges, with the Himalayan range extending into the country's north, where Hkakabo Razi reaches 19,296 feet as the highest point [8]. Major rivers including the Ayeyarwady (Irrawaddy), Chindwin, and Salween have historically shaped settlement patterns and economic activity [6]. The country maintains coastlines along the Bay of Bengal and Andaman Sea, providing geographic access to maritime trade routes [8].

Economic sectors traditionally centered on agriculture, natural resources, energy, border trade, and tourism, though current political instability has severely disrupted commercial infrastructure [6]. The ongoing crisis has created regional repercussions including rising transnational crime and continued economic volatility [4]. Traditional economic patterns built around river systems and agricultural production face unprecedented disruption under current conditions.

Myanmar's entertainment and cultural sectors operate within severe constraints imposed by the ongoing political crisis. The country's extensive festival calendar and cultural heritage sites continue to exist, though public gatherings and cultural expressions face restrictions under military rule. Traditional cultural practices among the diverse ethnic communities persist despite broader social and political disruptions affecting daily life across the country.

The intersection of cultural diversity and political repression creates particular challenges for minority communities, many of whom face both cultural suppression and direct violence. Cultural sites and practices that have developed over centuries now exist within a context of systematic rights violations and displacement affecting millions of people throughout the country.

• Myanmar's crisis has deepened five years after the military coup, with 5.2 million people displaced and widespread violations of international law reported by the UN
